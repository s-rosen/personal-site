ToBI in twenty minutes

Tones and Break Indices for Mainstream American English — a working primer, with your own recording as the worked example.

1 · The idea

ToBI (Tones and Break Indices) is the standard transcription system for English intonation, built on the autosegmental-metrical framework (Pierrehumbert 1980; conventions by Silverman, Beckman, Pitrelli, Ostendorf, Wightman, Price, Pierrehumbert & Hirschberg, 1992). Its central claim: a melody is not a continuous shape but a string of discrete tonal targets, each either H(igh) or L(ow), sparsely anchored to specific landmarks. Everything between targets is mere interpolation.

Three kinds of tonal events build a tune:

  • Pitch accents (marked with *) — anchored to the stressed syllable of prominent words.
  • Phrase accents (H-, L-) — control the pitch from the last accent to the edge of an intermediate phrase (ip).
  • Boundary tones (H%, L%, initial %H) — mark the edge of a full intonational phrase (IP).

Every IP needs at least one pitch accent, one phrase accent, and one boundary tone. Phrase accent + boundary tone arrive welded together at IP ends: L-L%, L-H%, H-H%, H-L%.

2 · Pitch accents

The star marks which tone sits on the stressed syllable. Shaded box = accented syllable.

LabelShapeSound & typical use
H* Plain peak on the stressed syllable. The default accent of declaratives; marks new information.
L* Valley on the stressed syllable. Prominent but low — yes/no questions (L* H-H%), backgrounded or non-asserted items.
L+H* Sharp rise from a genuine low up to a peak on the stressed syllable. Contrastive focus, correction ("no, UNcanny").
L*+H Low on the stressed syllable, rise arrives after it ("scooped"). Uncertainty, incredulity.
H+!H* High just before the stressed syllable, stepping down onto it. Implied familiarity; "as we both know."

3 · Downstep (!)

! before an H means that H is realized in a compressed pitch range — a terrace below the previous H in the same intermediate phrase. Chains of H* !H* !H*… create the descending staircase typical of neutral declaratives. Bitonal accents trigger downstep on what follows. Variants: !H*, L+!H*, L*+!H; H+!H* carries its downstep built in. Downstep resets at intermediate-phrase boundaries.

4 · Phrase accents and boundary tones

ComboShapeMeaning
L-L%Full final fall. Finished declarative. The default "period."
L-H%Fall then small final rise: the continuation rise — "I'm not done," list-internal comma.
H-H%High and rising further. Canonical yes/no question ending (pairs with L*).
H-L%Rise to a sustained plateau — calling contour, chanted lists, "more coming at the same level."
%HIP begins high — before any accent. Marks an energetic or linked-discourse onset.

A bare H- or L- (break 3) closes an intermediate phrase inside an IP — a smaller seam than a full boundary.

5 · Break indices

  • 0 — clitic boundary, words fused ("gonna").
  • 1 — ordinary word boundary. The default.
  • 2 — a mismatch: pause without tonal closure, or closure without pause.
  • 3 — intermediate phrase boundary; needs a phrase accent.
  • 4 — full intonational phrase boundary; needs phrase accent + boundary tone.

7 · Labeling pitfalls (earned the hard way)

  • Ears first, track second. The f0 track is evidence, not truth. If you hear a fall and the track shows a 60 ms uptick, trust the ear (creak and glottalization fool trackers).
  • Segmental perturbation: f0 spikes 20–40 ms after voiceless obstruents (/s/, /θ/, /k/…) are articulatory, not intonational. Your "solving" and "synthetic" both had them.
  • Octave errors: isolated doubling/halving islands in the track (your "uncanny" 447 Hz spike) are tracker artifacts.
  • Label sparsely. Not every word is accented; prenuclear material often carries nothing. When in doubt between H* and L+H*, ask whether the low before the peak is a real target or just where the voice happened to be.
  • The star goes on the stressed syllable, even when the peak is displaced later (peak delay is common; the label placement should still index the accented syllable).
